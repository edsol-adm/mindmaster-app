## In-Class-Game 1A ‚Äì Mind Parts Detective

**Lesson:** 01.01 - How Our Minds Work  

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

**Objective:** Identify the three parts of the mind-Thinking, Feeling, and Doing-and give one example of each from daily life. Recognize which part of the mind is active in common scenarios.

### Game File Name: icg-01.01-game01.html

### Game Title: ****Mind Part Detective****

### Game Format: ****Classification Trees / Gallery****

**Format Justification:** This format has 0 uses across all units and is ideal for teaching students to systematically categorize mental experiences into the three mind parts (Thinking, Feeling, Doing). The gallery/tree structure allows students to observe multiple examples and classify them step-by-step, building pattern recognition skills essential for understanding the mind framework. Unlike simple matching, this format requires deliberate sorting through categories, reinforcing the distinct characteristics of each mind part through repeated classification decisions.

### Purpose: Students observe everyday scenarios and classify them into the three mind parts (Idea Factory, Messenger, Action Player), strengthening their ability to distinguish between thinking, feeling, and doing through systematic categorization practice

### Lesson Arc Location****:**** Part C of Lesson 1 (approximately minutes 17-27 in the 45-minute lesson)

**Pedagogical Rationale:** This game is placed immediately after students have been introduced to the three mind parts in Part A and watched the Inside Out video demonstration. At this point, students understand the basic concept but need practice distinguishing between the three categories. The Classification Trees format provides structured, repetitive practice in categorization-the core skill needed before advancing to understanding reciprocal loops in Part D. By requiring students to sort multiple examples through a gallery interface, they build fluency with the framework vocabulary and develop pattern recognition that will support all future psychology learning.

### Setup

**Starting Screen:**

- Background: Bright, clean laboratory setting with three large sorting stations visible
- Title at top: "Mind Part Detective Challenge"
- Subtitle: "Help sort these everyday moments into the right mind part!"
- Three labeled stations displayed horizontally across screen:
  - **Station 1:** "IDEA FACTORY" (with lightbulb icon, yellow color theme)
  - **Station 2:** "MESSENGER" (with envelope/signal icon, blue color theme)
  - **Station 3:** "ACTION PLAYER" (with running figure icon, green color theme)
- Center of screen: Empty sorting area with text "Click START to begin sorting!"
- Bottom: "START SORTING" button (Button 5 on clicker)

**First Interaction:** Student presses Button 5 (START) to begin the challenge. First scenario card appears in center sorting area.

### Play (Step-by-Step Sequence)

**Round Structure:**  
Game presents 12 scenario cards one at a time. Each card must be sorted into the correct mind part station.

**For Each Scenario Card:**

- **Scenario Presentation:**
  - Card appears in center of screen with simple illustration + text description
  - Voice reads scenario aloud (auto-plays)
  - Three station options remain visible at top of screen
  - Student uses clicker to select which mind part:
    - Button 1 = IDEA FACTORY (Thinking)
    - Button 2 = MESSENGER (Feeling)
    - Button 3 = ACTION PLAYER (Doing)
- **Immediate Feedback:**
  - **If CORRECT:** Card animates smoothly into the correct station with a satisfying "click" sound
    - Text appears: "Correct! \[Scenario\] is \[Mind Part Name\] because \[brief explanation\]."
    - Example: "Correct! Planning what to do at recess is IDEA FACTORY because your mind is making a plan."
  - **If INCORRECT:** Card shakes gently, stays in center
    - Text appears: "Not quite. Think about whether this happens inside your mind or if others can see it."
    - Student gets second attempt (can press any of the 3 buttons again)
    - If incorrect on second attempt, card automatically sorts to correct station with explanation
- **Next Scenario:**
  - After feedback (3 seconds), next scenario card appears
  - Progress indicator shows: "Card \[X\] of 12"

**12 Scenario Cards:**

- **"Planning what game to play at recess"** ‚Üí IDEA FACTORY
- **"Feeling excited in your stomach"** ‚Üí MESSENGER
- **"Raising your hand in class"** ‚Üí ACTION PLAYER
- **"Remembering your friend's name"** ‚Üí IDEA FACTORY
- **"Smiling at someone"** ‚Üí ACTION PLAYER
- **"Feeling worried before a test"** ‚Üí MESSENGER
- **"Walking to the door"** ‚Üí ACTION PLAYER
- **"Imagining a flying elephant"** ‚Üí IDEA FACTORY
- **"Feeling happy in your chest"** ‚Üí MESSENGER
- **"Saying 'thank you' out loud"** ‚Üí ACTION PLAYER
- **"Solving a puzzle in your mind"** ‚Üí IDEA FACTORY
- **"Feeling nervous butterflies"** ‚Üí MESSENGER

**Final Round - Challenge Mode:**  
After completing 12 scenarios, game presents 3 "tricky" scenarios that students must sort without a second attempt:

- **"Choosing to wait quietly"** ‚Üí ACTION PLAYER (tricky because it's stillness, but it's observable choice)
- **"Wondering what's for lunch"** ‚Üí IDEA FACTORY (tricky because it feels passive)
- **"Feeling tired in your body"** ‚Üí MESSENGER (tricky because it's physical sensation)

### Scoring

**Points System:**

- **Correct on first try:** 2 points + card sorts into station
- **Correct on second try:** 1 point + card sorts into station
- **Incorrect both tries:** 0 points, but card still sorts correctly (for learning)

**Total Possible Points:** 27 points

**Completion:**  
Students complete all 15 scenario cards. Game declares completion when final card is sorted.

**Success Tiers:**

- **20+ points:** "Expert Mind Detective! You can tell the three mind parts apart!"
- **15-19 points:** "Good Mind Detective! You're learning the three mind parts well!"
- **10-14 points:** "Mind Detective in Training! Keep practicing to spot the differences!"
- **0-9 points:** "Keep exploring! The three mind parts take practice to learn!"

**End Screen:**

- Displays student's total points and tier message

**Link to Prior Learning:**

**Link to Future Learning:**  
This classification practice prepares students for Lesson 2 (exploring Thinking jobs in detail), Lesson 3 (exploring Feeling messages in detail), and Lesson 4 (understanding how the three parts work together in reciprocal loops). Fluency in categorizing mental experiences is foundational for all subsequent psychology learning.

## LESSON 2: Thinking - Our Mind's Idea Factory

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

Identify at least three different types of thinking their Idea Factory does (e.g., remembering, imagining, planning, problem-solving, wondering, deciding) and give an example of each from their own experience. Apply deliberate thinking to solve a simple problem by generating multiple possible solutions before choosing one.

### Game File Name: icg-01.02-game01.html

### Game Title: ****Idea Factory Job Matcher****

### Game Format: ****Matching Pairs (Memory Flip Game)****

**Format Justification:**  
This format has 0 uses across all units and is ideal for teaching students to recognize and match the six different thinking jobs (remembering, imagining, planning, problem-solving, wondering, deciding) with concrete examples from daily life. The memory flip mechanic requires students to hold multiple thinking jobs in working memory while searching for matches-itself a metacognitive exercise that reinforces the lesson's focus on how the Idea Factory works. Unlike simple identification, this format demands active retrieval and comparison, strengthening students' ability to distinguish between similar-seeming thinking types through repeated exposure and matching practice.

### Purpose: Students match thinking job labels (remembering, imagining, planning, problem-solving, wondering, deciding) with everyday scenario examples, strengthening their ability to recognize different types of thinking and understand that the Idea Factory performs multiple distinct jobs

### Lesson Arc Location:****:**** Part C of Lesson 2 (approximately minutes 25-35 in the 45-minute lesson)

**Pedagogical Rationale:** This game is placed after students have been introduced to the five main thinking jobs in Part A and completed the Memory List Test experiment in Part B. At this point, students have learned the vocabulary (remembering, imagining, planning, problem-solving, deciding) but need practice connecting these abstract labels to concrete, relatable situations. The Matching Pairs format provides intensive retrieval practice-students must recall what each thinking job means while searching for matching examples, which deepens their understanding through active mental work. This consolidation prepares them for the final reflection activity where they'll identify thinking jobs in picture scenarios, and builds the foundation for recognizing these thinking types in themselves and others throughout the curriculum.

## In-Class-Game 1C ‚Äì Messenger Message Decoder

## LESSON 3: Feeling - Our Mind's Messenger

## LESSON LINK & LEARNING OBJECTIVE

**Learning Objective:** Students will learn that feelings are information signals that communicate important data about ourselves and our situations, that feelings manifest both mentally (knowing the feeling) and physically (body sensations), and that different people can have different feelings about the same situation. Students will identify feelings by name, locate them in the body, and distinguish between feelings as information versus instructions.

## GAME FILE NAME: icg-01.03-game01.html

## GAME TITLE: Messenger Message Decoder

## GAME FORMAT: ****Sound & Mood Matching + Interactive Body Heat Map****

This format combines:

- Multi-sensory feeling recognition (audio cues paired with visual scenarios)
- Body mapping where students identify physical locations of emotions
- Pattern recognition across different scenarios showing how feelings provide consistent information
- Interactive heat map visualization showing where feelings "live" in the body

**Why this format:** This is clickable-compatible (students press buttons to match sounds to feelings, select body locations, and identify feeling messages). It's more complex than a simple emotion-matching quiz because it integrates multiple sensory inputs (audio + visual) and requires students to connect abstract feelings to concrete physical sensations. The body mapping component makes invisible feelings tangible and observable, reinforcing psychology as a science that studies measurable phenomena. The multi-modal approach (hearing, seeing, locating) creates deeper engagement and learning.

## LOCATION IN LESSON ARC: Part C of the lesson (approx 20 minutes)

**Replaces:** The existing "MindMaster Game - Messenger Message Decoder"

**Timing:** 10 minutes of gameplay

**Pedagogical Justification:** This placement occurs after students have:

- Learned the five core feeling-messages in Part A (fear=warning, sad=loss, angry=unfair, happy=good, worried=uncertain)
- Watched the "Feelings in My Body" video showing physical manifestation of emotions
- Completed the Body Mapping psychology experiment where they identified where they personally feel emotions

At this point, students understand WHAT feelings communicate and have begun noticing WHERE feelings appear physically. The game provides intensive practice connecting feeling-messages to body sensations through multiple sensory channels. The audio component adds a dimension not available in the psychology experiment, training students to recognize emotional tone in voices-a critical real-world skill. This multi-sensory practice deepens the understanding that feelings are both mental AND physical information signals that can be scientifically observed and decoded.

## PURPOSE

Students develop multi-sensory feeling recognition by matching emotional sounds to body locations and feeling-messages, building an understanding that feelings are physical, information-carrying signals that can be observed and decoded scientifically. The game trains students to recognize that feelings provide consistent, predictable information across different situations and people-reinforcing psychology's core principle that mental processes follow observable patterns.

## PLAY

### GAME STRUCTURE

The game consists of 4 missions progressing through different decoding challenges:

- **Mission 1:** Sound Signal Decoder (match sounds to feelings)
- **Mission 2:** Body Signal Locator (identify where feelings appear physically)
- **Mission 3:** Message Information Decoder (identify what information the feeling carries)
- **Mission 4:** Complete Decoder Challenge (integrate all skills)

Each mission builds on previous skills, creating progressive complexity.

### MISSION 1: SOUND SIGNAL DECODER (2.5 minutes)

**Mission Brief:**

- "MISSION 1: Feelings send signals through SOUNDS. Voices, music, and tones carry emotional information. Let's decode them!"

**Round Format:** Students hear 6 different audio clips and identify which feeling each sound represents.

**Sound 1: Excited Voice (10 seconds)**

- Audio plays: Child's voice saying "Look! Look! I did it! I really did it!" (excited, energetic tone)
- Question: "Which feeling message is this sound carrying?"
- Options displayed:
  - Press 1: SCARED
  - Press 2: SAD
  - Press 3: HAPPY/EXCITED
  - Press 4: ANGRY
  - Press 5: WORRIED
- **CORRECT: 3**
- Feedback: "YES! That sound carried HAPPY/EXCITED - the 'good thing happening' message! You decoded it!"

**Sound 2: Worried Voice (10 seconds)**

- Audio plays: Child's voice saying "I don't know... what if it doesn't work? What if..." (hesitant, uncertain tone)
- Question: "Which feeling message?"
- Same 5 options (press 1-5)
- **CORRECT: 5 (WORRIED)**
- Feedback: "Correct! That sound carried WORRIED - the 'unsure what happens' message!"

**Sound 3: Sad Music (10 seconds)**

- Audio plays: Slow, gentle piano music in minor key
- Question: "Even without words, music carries feeling messages! Which feeling?"
- Same 5 options

## In-Class-Game 1D ‚Äì Loop Power Discovery Lab

**LESSON 4: Doing - Our Mind's Action Player**

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

Explain how the three mind parts influence each other in reciprocal loops using specific examples with bidirectional connections. Demonstrate understanding of "loop power" by describing or showing a moment where they used their Action Player to influence their thinking or feeling through the feedback loop.

### Game File Name: icg-01.04-game01.html

### Game Title: ****Loop Power Discovery Lab****

### Game Format: ****Reverse Engineer****

**Format Justification:**  
This format has 0 uses across all units and is uniquely suited to teaching reciprocal loops and "loop power." Unlike forward-cause-and-effect formats, Reverse Engineer requires students to work backward from an observed outcome to identify which prior actions created that result-the essence of understanding loop power. Students see a final mental/emotional state and must trace backward through the feedback loop to discover which Action Player choice created the change in Thinking or Feeling. This backward reasoning reinforces the key lesson concept: that actions aren't just results of thoughts/feelings, but can be deliberately chosen to influence thoughts/feelings through feedback loops. The format makes the abstract concept of bidirectional influence concrete by requiring students to trace the causal chain in reverse.

### Purpose: Students observe final mental or emotional states and work backward to identify which Action Player choices created those outcomes through feedback loops, discovering that they have "loop power" to deliberately influence their thinking and feeling by choosing different actions

### Lesson Arc Location: Part C of Lesson 4 (approximately minutes 30-38 in the 45-minute lesson)

**Pedagogical Rationale:** This game is placed after students have completed the three-round Psychology Experiment demonstrating reciprocal loops (Thinking‚ÜíDoing, Doing‚ÜíFeeling, and complete loops) and watched the body language video in Part B. At this point, students have experienced that actions can change feelings and thoughts, but they need practice deliberately identifying and tracing these loops backward. The Reverse Engineer format provides exactly this-by starting with an outcome and working backward, students must actively think about which action created the change, reinforcing their agency and "loop power." This backward-tracing skill is the practical application of reciprocal loop understanding: recognizing that they can choose actions strategically to influence their mental states. The game prepares students for the reflection activity where they'll identify their own loop power moments and consolidates the unit's culminating concept that actions are both controllable and powerful tools for influencing the entire mind system.

### Play (Step-by-Step Sequence)

**Game Structure:**  
Game presents 10 reverse-engineering investigations. Each shows a FINAL STATE (outcome) and students must identify which ACTION created that change by working backward through the loop.

**Investigation Format:**

- **Scenario Presentation:**
  - Display monitor shows final outcome with illustration and text
  - Format: "FINAL STATE: \[Character name\] now feels/thinks \[outcome\]. What ACTION created this change?"
  - Voice narration reads scenario
  - Brief animation shows the final state (e.g., character smiling, standing tall, looking confident)
- **The Backward Trace Challenge:**
  - Three action options appear (clicker buttons 1-3)
  - Each option represents a different Action Player choice that might have preceded the outcome
  - Student must select which action created the final state through a feedback loop
  - Timer (optional): 15 seconds to choose (gentle, non-stressful)
- **Answer Feedback:**

**If CORRECT:**

- - Loop diagram animates on screen showing the bidirectional path:
        - ACTION ‚Üí influenced ‚Üí FEELING/THINKING ‚Üí created feedback ‚Üí NEW OUTCOME
    - Feedback text: "Correct! Working backward: \[Character\] DID \[action\], which changed \[their feeling/thinking\], creating loop power!"
      - Example: "Correct! Working backward: Maya DID smile and wave, which made her FEEL friendly, which made her THINK 'I'm brave,' creating loop power!"
    - Character gives thumbs up
    - +10 points awarded
    - "LOOP DISCOVERED!" badge briefly appears

**If INCORRECT:**

- - Screen shows gentle shake
    - Feedback: "Not quite. That action wouldn't create this outcome. Think: what did \[character\] DO with their body that could change how they felt or thought?"
    - Second attempt available: remaining two options shown
    - If correct on second try: +5 points, loop diagram shown with explanation
    - If incorrect again: Correct answer revealed with loop explanation, 0 points

- **Next Investigation:**

### Scoring

**Points System:**

- **Correct on first attempt:** 10 points + "LOOP DISCOVERED!" badge
- **Correct on second attempt:** 5 points + loop explanation
- **Incorrect both attempts:** 0 points, but correct answer shown with explanation (for learning)

**Total Possible Points:** 100 points (10 investigations √ó 10 points each)

**Loop Power Meter:**

- Visual meter fills throughout game as points accumulate
- Meter labeled with three zones:
  - 0-40 points: "Discovering Loop Power" (bronze zone)
  - 50-70 points: "Using Loop Power" (silver zone)
  - 80-100 points: "Loop Power Expert" (gold zone)
- Meter provides visual feedback on understanding level

**Running Display:**

- Top right: Current points total
- Top center: "Investigations: \[X\]/10 completed"
- Left side: Loop Power Meter filling progressively

### Win Condition

**Completion:**  
Students complete all 10 reverse-engineering investigations. Game advances to end screen after final investigation feedback.

**End Screen Display:**

**Success Message Based on Points:**

- **80-100 points (Loop Power Expert):** "Loop Power Expert! You discovered how the Action Player creates powerful feedback loops that change thinking and feeling!"
- **50-70 points (Using Loop Power):** "Loop Power User! You're learning how actions create feedback that changes your mind!"
- **0-40 points (Discovering Loop Power):** "Loop Power Discoverer! You're starting to understand how your Action Player influences the whole mind system!"

**Summary Visualization:**

- Large Loop Power Meter showing final level with zone color (bronze/silver/gold)
- "You traced \[X\] loops correctly on the first try!"
- Animated loop diagram showing all three mind parts with bidirectional arrows
- Key insight text box: "Remember: You can't always control thoughts and feelings appearing, BUT you CAN control your actions-and your actions create feedback that influences your thoughts and feelings. That's your LOOP POWER!"

**Reflection Challenge:**

- Text: "Think about TODAY in this class. Can you remember a time when YOU used loop power?"
- Prompt: "What did your Action Player DO that changed how you felt or thought?"
- Input: Four example options (clicker buttons 1-4):
  - I smiled and it made me feel happier
  - I sat up tall and felt more confident
  - I tried something and felt proud
  - I took a breath and felt calmer
- All answers accepted as valid with response: "Yes! That's YOUR loop power working! You used your Action Player to influence your whole mind!"

**Exit:**

- Button 5: "RETURN TO LESSON" (links back to lesson-01.04.html)

**Class Summary (Teacher View):**

- Average score and zone distribution
- Most challenging investigation (highest error rate)
- Percentage scoring 80+ (Loop Power Expert level)
- Distribution of reflection responses (which loop power strategy students recalled using)

### Continuity Note

**Link to Prior Learning:**  
This game integrates all three mind parts from Lessons 1-3 (Thinking/Idea Factory, Feeling/Messenger, Doing/Action Player) and demonstrates the reciprocal loop model introduced throughout Lesson 4. Students apply their understanding that the three parts influence each other bidirectionally, with special focus on how actions create feedback.

**Link to Future Learning:**  
Understanding loop power-that controllable actions can influence thoughts and feelings-is foundational for all future units. This concept will support learning about attention strategies (Unit 2), memory techniques (Unit 3), emotion understanding (Unit 4), relationship skills (Unit 5-6), and problem-solving (Unit 7-10). The ability to trace loops backward develops metacognitive awareness and empowers students to recognize their agency within the mind system throughout the curriculum.

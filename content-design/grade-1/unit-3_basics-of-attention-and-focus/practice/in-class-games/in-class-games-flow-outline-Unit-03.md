## In-Class-Game 3A â€“ Spotlight Speed Challenge

# LESSON 1: Attention - the Mind's Spotlight

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

- Define attention as the mind's spotlight that selects what to notice from all available information
- Explain how attention determines what gets learned and remembered
- Distinguish between strong attention (spotlight focused) and wandering attention (spotlight drifting)
- Recognize that attention is a skill that can be understood, trained, and used

### Game File Name: icg-03.01-game01.html

### **Game Title: **Spotlight Speed Challenge****

### Game Format: **Reaction Time Challenge with Progressive Difficulty**

This format uses timed visual detection tasks that increase in complexity, measuring both speed and accuracy to demonstrate attention as an observable, measurable cognitive function.

### Purpose: This game demonstrates attention as the mind's spotlight through direct experience. Children practice directing their attention to target stimuli while ignoring distractions, experiencing firsthand how focused attention works and how it can be measured. The game provides concrete evidence that attention is selective (you can't notice everything at once) and variable (performance changes based on focus strength)

**Location in lesson arc**: Explore + Practice section, Practice A. Minutes 10-20 (after video, before worksheet)

**Pedagogical Justification**: Positioned immediately after the video on attention as selective process so students can experience the spotlight metaphor firsthand through timed detection tasks. Generates personal performance data (speed/accuracy) that validates the concept before worksheet activities require identifying focused vs. wandering attention in scenarios.

### Setup

**Opening Screen:**

- **Title:** "Spotlight Speed Challenge"
- **Character:** Friendly cartoon owl character named "Scout" wearing scientist goggles (will be reused in Lesson 3 game to connect lessons on attention as trainable)
- **Visual:** Scout holds a flashlight/spotlight beam
- **Instruction Text:** "Your attention is like a spotlight! It shines on one thing at a time. Let's test how fast your spotlight can find targets. Get your clicker ready!"

**Pre-Game Instructions (Screen 2):**

- **Visual:** Split screen showing:
  - LEFT: 9 mixed shapes (circles, squares, triangles) in various colors
  - RIGHT: Scout's spotlight highlighting one red circle with a glowing effect
- **Text:** "Watch the screen carefully. When you see a RED CIRCLE, press Button 5 as fast as you can!"
- **Practice Prompt:** "Let's try 3 practice rounds first."

**Practice Rounds (3 rounds):**

- Simple displays with 4-5 shapes
- 1 red circle clearly visible
- Feedback: "Great spotting!" or "Try again - look for the red circle"
- No scoring during practice
- After practice: "Ready for the real challenge? Your spotlight is warmed up!"

### Play

**Game Structure: 3 Levels with Increasing Difficulty**

**LEVEL 1: BASIC SPOTLIGHT (Rounds 1-5)**

**Round Setup:**

- Display shows 6-7 shapes arranged in loose cluster
- Colors: Blue, yellow, green, red
- Target: 1 red circle (sometimes 2)
- No distracting elements
- Display time: 3 seconds per round
- Response window: Children press Button 5 when they spot red circle(s)

**Feedback Loop:**

- **Correct detection within 2 seconds:** "Fast spotlight! +10 points" (green checkmark appears)
- **Correct detection 2-3 seconds:** "Good spotting! +7 points" (yellow checkmark)
- **Missed or incorrect:** "Keep your spotlight focused!" (gentle neutral tone, no points deducted)

**Between Rounds:**

- Brief 1-second transition showing Scout's spotlight beam "recharging"
- Round counter: "Round 2 of 5"

**LEVEL 2: DISTRACTION CHALLENGE (Rounds 6-10)**

**Round Setup:**

- Display shows 8-10 shapes in tighter arrangement
- Colors: Blue, yellow, green, red, orange, purple
- Target: 1-2 red circles
- **New element:** Some shapes now pulse or wobble (visual distraction)
- **New element:** 1-2 red squares or triangles appear (color match but wrong shape)
- Display time: 3 seconds per round
- Children must still press Button 5 only for red circles

**Increased Cognitive Demand:**

- Selective attention: Ignore red non-circles
- Sustained attention: Maintain focus despite movement
- Visual filtering: Block out distracting animations

**Feedback Loop:**

- **Correct within 2 seconds:** "Strong spotlight! Ignored distractions! +15 points"
- **Correct 2-3 seconds:** "Good focus! +10 points"
- **Pressed for red square/triangle:** "Almost! Remember - red CIRCLES only. Your spotlight almost had it!"
- **Missed:** "Distractions are tricky. Keep practicing your spotlight!"

**Level Transition (after Round 10):**

- **Screen:** Scout gives thumbs up
- **Text:** "Your spotlight is getting stronger! Ready for the final challenge?"
- **Button Prompt:** "Press any button to continue"

**LEVEL 3: SPEED SPOTLIGHT (Rounds 11-15)**

**Round Setup:**

- Display shows 10-12 shapes in complex arrangement
- Full rainbow of colors
- Target: 2-3 red circles mixed throughout
- Multiple distractors (red shapes, pulsing elements)
- **New challenge:** Display time reduced to 2.5 seconds
- Children press Button 5 for EACH red circle they spot

**Maximum Difficulty:**

- Tests sustained selective attention
- Requires rapid spotlight shifting between multiple targets
- High visual noise environment

**Feedback Loop:**

- **Found all red circles within 2 seconds:** "Spotlight expert! All targets found! +20 points"
- **Found all circles 2-2.5 seconds:** "Excellent attention! +15 points"
- **Found some circles:** "Good spotting! Found \[X\] of \[Y\] circles. +10 points"
- **Missed all:** "This one was very tricky! Your spotlight tried hard."

**Final Round (Round 15) - Special:**

- **Text appears before display:** "Final challenge! This one is the trickiest yet!"
- Extra complex display with 3 red circles hidden among 15 shapes
- Bonus points available: +25 for finding all within 2 seconds

**GAME COMPLETION SEQUENCE**

**Results Screen:**

- **Visual:** Scout holding a clipboard with bar graph showing:
  - Level 1 average speed
  - Level 2 average speed
  - Level 3 average speed
- **Stats Displayed:**
  - Total Score: \[XXX\] points
  - Total Red Circles Found: \[XX\] of 25 possible
  - Fastest Spotlight Time: \[X.X\] seconds
  - Accuracy: \[XX\]%

**Performance Badge:**

- **0-100 points:** "Spotlight Learner" - Bronze spotlight badge
- **101-200 points:** "Spotlight Tracker" - Silver spotlight badge
- **201-300 points:** "Spotlight Master" - Gold spotlight badge
- **301+ points:** "Spotlight Scientist" - Platinum spotlight with stars

**Educational Message:** "Scientists measure attention by testing speed and accuracy - just like you did! Your spotlight got stronger with practice. Notice how hard it was to see everything at once? That's because attention picks ONE thing to shine on. Great scientific work!"

### Scoring

**Point System:**

- **Level 1 (Basic):**
  - Fast detection (<2 sec): 10 points
  - Slower detection (2-3 sec): 7 points
  - Maximum per round: 10 points
- **Level 2 (Distraction):**
  - Fast selective attention (<2 sec): 15 points
  - Slower selective attention (2-3 sec): 10 points
  - Maximum per round: 15 points
- **Level 3 (Speed):**
  - Found all targets fast (<2 sec): 20 points
  - Found all targets slower (2-2.5 sec): 15 points
  - Found partial targets: 10 points
  - Round 15 bonus possible: 25 points

**Maximum Possible Score:** 325 points

- Level 1: 50 points (5 rounds Ã— 10)
- Level 2: 75 points (5 rounds Ã— 15)
- Level 3: 100 points (4 rounds Ã— 20, plus round 15 Ã— 25)

**Scoring Philosophy:** Speed + accuracy demonstrates attention strength. Progressive difficulty shows that attention faces increasing challenges. Individual improvement data (comparing early vs. late rounds) demonstrates attention as trainable.

### Win Condition

**Completion-Based with Tiered Success:**

- **Complete all 15 rounds** = Game finished
- **Earn performance badge** based on total points
- **No failure state** - all attempts contribute to learning

**Success Metrics Displayed:**

- Total score (quantitative measure of attention performance)
- Accuracy percentage (quality of selective attention)
- Speed improvement (comparing early vs. late rounds)
- Badge earned (recognition of achievement level)

**Educational Win:** Children understand that attention can be observed and measured, that it improves with practice, and that it's selective by nature (can't see everything at once).

### Continuity Note

**Connection to Lesson 1:** This game extends the "attention as spotlight" metaphor introduced in the lesson warm-up activity. While the in-class warm-up used a physical frame to demonstrate selective attention with a static image, this game demonstrates the same principle through dynamic, timed challenges that require active spotlight control.

**Bridge to Lesson 2:** The three difficulty levels subtly introduce the three types of attention that will be explicitly taught in Lesson 2:

- Level 1 = Sustained attention (maintaining focus across rounds)
- Level 2 = Selective attention (filtering distractors)
- Level 3 = Shifting attention (moving between multiple targets)

Children experience these attention modes practically before learning their formal names.

**Connection to Unit Arc:** Establishes baseline understanding that attention is measurable and observable - a key principle for the scientific framework. The performance data (speed, accuracy, improvement) provides concrete evidence that attention is not mysterious but quantifiable.

# LESSON 2: The Different Kinds of Attention

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

- Identify the three types of attention-sustained (staying focused), selective (blocking distractions), and shifting (changing focus)-and give one example of each
- Match attention types to appropriate situations
- Recognize which type of attention they're using during different activities
- Explain that the mind has different "spotlight modes" for different purposes

### Game File Name: **icg-03.02-game01.html**

### **Game Title: **Attention Mode Matcher****

### Game Format: **Interactive Scenario Sorting with Contextual Reasoning**

This format presents realistic scenarios through animated vignettes and requires children to analyze which attention type is being used, moving beyond simple memorization to contextual understanding and application.

### Purpose: This game builds deep understanding of the three attention types by requiring children to identify them in realistic, relatable scenarios. Rather than matching definitions, children must analyze what's happening in each situation and determine which attention mode would work best. This develops both recognition skills (identifying attention types in action) and strategic thinking (knowing when to use each type)

**Location in lesson arc**: Explore + Practice section, Practice A. Minutes 10-23 (after tutorial, before observation task).

**Pedagogical Justification**: Positioned after character tutorial to provide 18 practice scenarios for classification before students attempt the observation task in Practice B. Progressive difficulty (obvious â†’ mixed â†’ application) builds competence systematically, ensuring students can confidently categorize attention types in real-world observations.

- **Title:** "Attention Mode Matcher"
- **Characters:** Three cartoon animal friends representing each attention type:
  - **Steady the Turtle** (Sustained attention) - wearing a headband, looking focused on a puzzle

# **LESSON 4: Attention Works Differently for Everyone**

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

- Recognize that attention patterns vary across people
- Identify factors that influence attention for themselves and others (energy, interest, noise, time of day)
- Describe their own attention patterns and what conditions help them focus best
- Demonstrate respect for different attention needs by adjusting behavior or workspace
- Apply self-awareness by choosing strategies or environments that match attention needs

### Game File Name: icg-03.04-game01.html

### **Game Title: **Attention Pattern Detective****

### Game Format: **Observation-Based Pattern Recognition with Personal Reflection**

This format uses scenario analysis to identify attention patterns across different people and situations, followed by self-reflection to build awareness of personal attention characteristics. Rather than competitive scoring, success is measured through pattern identification and empathetic understanding.

### Purpose: This game builds understanding that attention variability is normal and functional, not deficient. Children observe different attention patterns across scenarios, identify factors that influence attention (environment, energy, interest), and reflect on their own patterns. This develops both self-awareness (knowing what helps their attention) and empathy (respecting that classmates have different needs). The game frames individual differences as scientific observation rather than judgment

**Location in lesson arc**: Explore + Practice section, spanning Practice A and B.

Phases 1-2: Minutes 12-20 (~8 minutes)

Phases 3-4: Minutes 20-35 (integrated with Practice B)

**Pedagogical Justification**: Split placement necessary because lesson requires both external observation and internal reflection. Phases 1-2 positioned after video to build pattern recognition skills through observation. Phase 3 integrated with Personal Profile worksheet for self-assessment. Phase 4 follows because students need both others' patterns and self-awareness before applying empathy to social scenarios.

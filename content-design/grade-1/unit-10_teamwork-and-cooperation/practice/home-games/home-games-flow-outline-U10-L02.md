### Learning Objective

Students will explain that strong teams distribute responsibility so everyone contributes, and will identify different valuable roles in teamwork (leading, organizing, creating, supporting).

### Game File Name

`hg-10.02-01.html`

### Game Format

Cause & Effect Chains (Science App Format)

Players arrange team roles in sequence to see how cooperation unfolds. This format teaches process thinking and shows the causal relationship between role distribution and team success.

### Game Purpose

Students practice sequencing team roles to understand that different roles work together in a coordinated process. They discover that all roles are necessary and that proper role distribution leads to successful outcomes.

### Estimated Play Time

8â€“10 minutes

**Opening Screen:**

- Start button: "Build Your Team"

**Instructions:**

- "Teams work best when everyone has a role. You'll see a team project. Drag the right roles into order to make the team succeed!"
- Visual guide showing four role cards:
    - ðŸŒŸ LEADER (gives direction)
    - ðŸ“‹ ORGANIZER (prepares materials)

### Play

**5 Team Projects (Increasing Complexity):**

Each project presents a scenario requiring students to sequence roles correctly.

**Project 1: Building a Block Tower**

- **Setup:** "A team wants to build a tower. What order should the roles happen?"
- **Available role cards (scrambled):** Leader, Organizer, Creator, Supporter
- **Correct sequence:**
    1.  Creator ("I have an ideaâ€”let's build it tall and wide!")
    2.  Organizer ("I'll get the blocks ready and sort them by size")
    3.  Leader ("Okay team, let's start with the base, then build up")
    4.  Supporter ("Great job everyone! You're doing amazing!")
- **Player action:** Drag role cards into 4 empty slots on conveyor belt
- **Feedback mechanism:**
    1.  If correct: Animated sequence plays showing team successfully building tower, with each avatar performing their role
    2.  If incorrect: Animation shows team confused, tower wobbles, team stops. Prompt: "Hmm, something's not quite right. Try a different order!"
- **After success:** "You discovered: Teams work smoothly when roles are in the right order!"

**Project 2: Making a Poster**

- **Setup:** "A team is making a poster for the classroom. Arrange the roles!"
- **Correct sequence:**
    1.  Leader ("Let's make a poster about animals. Everyone ready?")
    2.  Creator ("I have ideas for which animals to draw!")
    3.  Organizer ("I'll get paper, markers, and set up our space")
    4.  Supporter ("This poster is going to be great! Keep going!")
- **Outcome:** Poster appears beautifully completed

**Project 3: Putting on a Play**

- **Setup:** "A team wants to perform a play. What's the best order?"
- **Correct sequence:**
    1.  Creator ("Let's tell a story about a dragon and a knight!")
    2.  Leader ("Great! You be the dragon, I'll be the knight, and you can do the narration")
    3.  Organizer ("I'll set up costumes and props for everyone")
    4.  Supporter ("You're all doing such a good job rehearsing!")
- **Outcome:** Play performance animation plays

**Project 4: Cleaning Up the Classroom**

- **Setup:** "A team is cleaning up after art time. Sequence the roles!"
- **Correct sequence:**
    1.  Leader ("Let's split upâ€”some wash tables, some organize supplies")
    2.  Organizer ("I'll get sponges, bins, and cleaning supplies ready")
    3.  Creator ("What if we make a game out of it? Race to see who finishes first!")
    4.  Supporter ("Everyone's working so hard! Almost done!")
- **Outcome:** Clean classroom appears

**Project 5: Group Science Experiment**

- **Setup:** "A team is doing a science experiment. Put the roles in order!"
- **Correct sequence:**
    1.  Creator ("Let's see what happens when we mix these colors!")
    2.  Leader ("Good idea! First we'll mix red and blue, then add yellow")
    3.  Organizer ("I'll get cups, water, and color tablets for everyone")
    4.  Supporter ("This experiment is so cool! Great teamwork everyone!")
- **Outcome:** Colorful results animation

**Final Summary Screen:**

- "You completed all 5 projects!"
- Key discovery: "Every role is important. Leader, Organizer, Creator, and Supporter all make teams work!"
- Visual: All four role avatars standing together, holding a trophy

## HOME GAME 2B: Listen & Build

### Learning Objective

Students will demonstrate listening carefully to others' ideas and taking turns during cooperative activity, and will compare how teamwork breaks down when people don't listen versus how smoothly it works with fair participation.

### Game File Name

hg-10.02-02.html

### Game Format

**Simulation / Sandbox (Science App Format)**

Players manipulate the "listening level" variable in team scenarios and observe how it affects team outcomes. This simulation format reinforces cause-effect reasoning and scientific observation.

### Purpose

Students experiment with different levels of listening in team scenarios to discover that listening directly affects team success. They observe that teams function smoothly when members listen, and break down when they don't.

### Estimated Play Time

9â€“10 minutes

### Setup

**Opening Screen:**

- Title: "Listen & Build"
- Subtitle: "How does listening change teamwork?"
- Visual: Laboratory-style interface with control panel and observation window
- Start button: "Start Experiment"

**Instructions:**

- "You're going to run experiments! You'll control how much a team listens to each other. Then watch what happens!"
- Visual: Slider control labeled "Listening Level" with emoji faces from ðŸ˜¤ (not listening) to ðŸ˜Š (listening well)

### Play

**3 Team Scenarios (Experimental Format):**

Each scenario allows the player to set the listening level, observe the outcome, then adjust and try again.

**Scenario 1: Building a Block Castle**

- **Setup:** Four cartoon avatars appear in the observation window, ready to build
- **Control Panel:**
    - Listening slider (3 levels: Low / Medium / High)
    - "Run Experiment" button
- **Experiment A (Player sets to LOW listening):**
    - Animation plays:
        - Avatar 1: "Let's build wide!"
        - Avatar 2: "No! Tall is better!" (not listening to Avatar 1)
        - Avatar 3: "I want red blocks!" (ignoring others)
        - Avatar 4: "Wait, what are we doing?" (confused)
    - Outcome: Castle collapses. Team members look frustrated.
    - Data recorded: "Low listening = Castle fell apart. Team frustrated."
- **Experiment B (Player adjusts to HIGH listening):**
    - Animation plays:
    - Avatar 1: "Let's build wide!"
        - Avatar 2: "I hear you. What if we build wide AND add a tall tower?"
        - Avatar 3: "Good idea! I'll use red blocks for the tower."
        - Avatar 4: "I'll help with the base!"
    - Outcome: Beautiful castle appears. Team members high-five.
    - Data recorded: "High listening = Castle successful! Team happy."
- **Reflection prompt:** "What did you notice? How did listening change what happened?"
- Player clicks observations (multiple choice):
    - "When listening was low, people argued"
    - "When listening was high, people worked together"
    - "Listening helped the team succeed"
    - All correct; affirming feedback given

**Scenario 2: Making a Group Decision (What Game to Play)**

- **Setup:** Three avatars need to choose between three games
- **Control Panel:** Listening slider + "Run Experiment"
- **Experiment A (Player sets to LOW):**
    - Avatar 1: "Tag!"
    - Avatar 2: "No, hide and seek!"
    - Avatar 3: "Soccer!" (no one listening)
    - Outcome: No decision made. Everyone walks away separately. Timer runs out.
    - Data: "Low listening = No decision. Team split up."
- **Experiment B (Player sets to HIGH):**
    - Avatar 1: "I like tag!"
    - Avatar 2: "I hear you. I like hide and seek."
    - Avatar 3: "I like soccer. What if we vote?"
    - All avatars: "Good idea!"
    - Outcome: Vote happens, everyone agrees to play the winner (hide and seek). Team plays together happily.
    - Data: "High listening = Decision made. Team stayed together."

**Scenario 3: Solving a Puzzle Together**

- **Setup:** Four avatars have puzzle pieces to assemble
- **Control Panel:** Listening slider
- **Experiment A (Player sets to MEDIUM - introduces partial listening):**
    - Avatar 1: "This piece goes here"
    - Avatar 2: "Okay!" (places piece)
    - Avatar 3: "Wait, I have a better ideaâ€”" (interrupted)
    - Avatar 4: "Just do it my way!" (not listening fully)
    - Outcome: Puzzle completed, but took longer. One avatar looks left out.
    - Data: "Medium listening = Puzzle done, but not everyone felt included."
- **Experiment B (Player sets to HIGH):**
    - Avatar 1: "This piece goes here"
    - Avatar 2: "Good idea!"
    - Avatar 3: "Wait, I see a faster wayâ€”what if we sort by color first?"
    - Avatar 4: "Great thinking! Let's try that!"
    - Outcome: Puzzle completed quickly. All avatars celebrate together.
    - Data: "High listening = Puzzle done fast! Everyone felt important."

**Final Lab Report Screen:**

- "Experiment Complete! Here's what you discovered:"
- Graph showing: Listening Level (x-axis) vs. Team Success (y-axis)
- As listening increases, success increases (clear upward trend)
- Scientific conclusion: "Listening makes teams work better. When people listen, they cooperate smoothly. When they don't listen, teams struggle."

## How Lesson 2 Fits into the Full Learning Arc

Lesson 2 takes the foundation from Lesson 1â€”"teams can do more than one person"â€”and moves inside the machinery of teamwork. Through Home Game 2A (Role Factory), students see that cooperation is not just many hands working at once, but different roles working in a clear sequence: leading, organizing, creating, and supporting. Through Home Game 2B (Listen & Build), they test how listening acts as the "glue" that allows these roles to coordinate. Together, the two games turn teamwork from a vague idea into something concrete, observable, and controllable: roles can be shared, and listening levels can be adjusted like a slider.

* **Lesson 1:** Establishes that teamwork means combining efforts toward a shared goal, not just sitting near others. Students experience how adding teammates changes what is possible and learn to spot the difference between proximity and true cooperation.
* **Lesson 2 (this document):** Zooms in on *how* teams function on the inside. Home Game 2A shows that strong teams distribute responsibility across different roles and that each role contributes to success. Home Game 2B then demonstrates that listening and turn-taking determine whether those roles fit together smoothly or fall apart. Students begin to understand that they can shape teamwork by both *how they listen* and *which role they play*.
* **Lesson 3:** Builds on this by exploring what happens when teamwork goes wrongâ€”when roles clash, listening breaks down, or more than one person tries to take the same role. Students practise cooperative problem-solving and simple conflict-resolution strategies that repair team functioning rather than blame individuals.
* **Lesson 4:** Brings the arc to a close by focusing on shared recognition and celebration. Students learn to notice what the team achieved and to appreciate different contributionsâ€”leaders, organisers, creators, and supportersâ€”cementing the idea that success belongs to the group, not just the most visible role.

> ðŸ¤ **Learning Arc Summary:** Lesson 1 defines what teamwork is; Lesson 2 explains how it works from the inside (roles + listening); Lesson 3 tackles what to do when it breaks; Lesson 4 celebrates what teams can achieve together.
> By the end of Unit 10, students see themselves not only as helpers inside a team, but as *active shapers* of how their teams function. They understand that listening carefully and sharing roles fairly are skills they can use to make cooperation smoother in families, classrooms, and communities.

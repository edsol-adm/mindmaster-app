## In-Class-Game 8A ‚Äì Problem Size Sorter

**Lesson 1: Problems Are Part of Life**

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

Students will categorize problems by size (small, medium, larger) and recognize that problems are normal, everyday experiences that everyone faces.

### Game File Name: icg-08.01-game01.html

### Game Title: ****Problem Size Sorter****

### Game Format: ****Classification Trees / Gallery****

**Format Justification:** This format allows students to actively classify problems into categories through a step-by-step decision-making process, directly reinforcing the lesson's core objective of categorizing problems by size. The gallery metaphor positions students as "problem scientists" who observe, analyze, and systematically categorize phenomena-aligning with the psychology-as-science framework. Unlike passive sorting, the tree/gallery structure requires students to make explicit decisions at each classification node, building metacognitive awareness of what features determine problem size (how many people affected, how hard to solve, how much help needed). This active classification process mirrors how psychologists categorize and study problems scientifically, making abstract categorization concrete and observable.

### Purpose: Students practice categorizing everyday problems as small, medium, or larger by examining problem scenarios and selecting which size category fits best. This reinforces that problems can be observed, analyzed, and systematically classified-treating problem-solving as a science

### Placement in Lesson Arc: Practice C (minutes 28-33). AFTER Practice B (In-Class Experiment where students identified their own problems). **BEFORE:** Reflection

**Pedagogical Rationale:** By minute 28, students have been introduced to problem categories through hands-on sorting with peers (Practice A) and have identified problems from their own lives (Practice B). The game serves as consolidation and individual mastery check-students now apply the categorization skill independently with immediate feedback. This placement allows the teacher to assess whether students can reliably distinguish problem sizes before moving to reflection, while the 5-minute duration maintains energy and engagement as a final practice activity before wind-down.

### Setup

**Opening Screen:**

- Background: Bright, clean "Problem Science Lab" with three large display cases labeled "SMALL," "MEDIUM," and "LARGER"
- Character: Friendly scientist avatar (cartoon style, lab coat, magnifying glass) named "Dr. Wonder"
- Text bubble from Dr. Wonder: "Welcome to the Problem Lab! Scientists sort problems by size. Let's practice!"
- Button 5 (center): "Start Sorting"

**First Interaction:**  
Student clicks Button 5 to begin Round 1.

### Play

**Round Structure:** 8 rounds total  
Each round follows this sequence:

**Step 1: Problem Scenario Presentation**

- Screen displays a problem scenario with simple illustration
- Text appears in large, clear font
- Dr. Wonder asks: "What SIZE is this problem?"
- Three classification buttons appear:
  - Button 1: "SMALL"
  - Button 5: "MEDIUM"
  - Button 9: "LARGER"

**Step 2: Student Response**

- Student selects one button
- Response is recorded in MindMaster system

**Step 3: Feedback & Explanation**

- Correct choice: Display case for that size glows, problem card "files" into it
  - Feedback text: "You classified it! \[Brief reason why it's that size\]"
- Incorrect choice: Gentle shake animation, no sound
  - Feedback text: "Look again. Think about: How many people? How hard to solve?"
  - Student can try again (no penalty)

**Step 4: Classification Reasoning**

- After correct answer, Dr. Wonder explains ONE classification clue:
  - Small problems: "This affects just one person and can be fixed quickly."
  - Medium problems: "This involves more than one person or needs some thinking."
  - Larger problems: "This affects many people or needs lots of help and time."

**Step 5: Next Round**

- Button 5: "Next Problem"
- Advances to next scenario

**Problem Scenarios (8 total):**

- **Scenario 1 (SMALL):** "You can't find your pencil."  
    Illustration: Child looking at empty desk
- **Scenario 2 (SMALL):** "Your shoelace is untied."  
    Illustration: Shoe with loose lace
- **Scenario 3 (MEDIUM):** "You and your friend both want to play with the same toy."  
    Illustration: Two children reaching for one toy
- **Scenario 4 (MEDIUM):** "You can't remember where you put your homework."  
    Illustration: Child looking puzzled, papers scattered

    Illustration: Child standing alone near unfamiliar building

    Illustration: Small puddle on desk

    Illustration: Two children turned away from each other, third child in middle
- **Scenario 8 (MEDIUM):** "You want to build something but don't have all the pieces you need."  
    Illustration: Child with incomplete building blocks

**Classification Decision Tree Logic:**

The game uses implicit decision nodes students consider:

- **Node 1:** "How many people does this affect?" (1 person ‚Üí lean Small; 2+ people ‚Üí lean Medium/Larger)
- **Node 2:** "How much help or time is needed?" (Quick fix ‚Üí Small; Some thinking ‚Üí Medium; Lots of help ‚Üí Larger)
- **Node 3:** "How hard is it to solve?" (Easy ‚Üí Small; Takes work ‚Üí Medium; Very challenging ‚Üí Larger)

Students don't see these nodes explicitly but the feedback after each round highlights which factor made it that size.

## In-Class-Game 8B ‚Äì Problem Path Builder

## Lesson 2: Steps for Solving Problems

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

Students will learn and apply a clear, four-step problem-solving framework: Stop and Notice ‚Üí Think of Options ‚Üí Choose One ‚Üí Try It Out. They will understand that following steps makes problem-solving feel less overwhelming and that the goal is to have a plan to try, not to find a perfect answer immediately.

### Game File Name: icg-08.02-game01.html

### **Game Title: **Problem Path Builder****

### **Game Format**: **Story Path Vote**

****Format Justification**:** This format allows students to experience the four-step problem-solving process as an unfolding journey where they make sequential decisions at critical choice points. Unlike static instruction, the story path structure demonstrates that problem-solving is a dynamic process where each step builds on the previous one and leads to observable outcomes. Students vote at each decision node (Stop and Notice ‚Üí Think of Options ‚Üí Choose One ‚Üí Try It Out), experiencing how following the systematic process leads to resolution while skipping steps or choosing poorly leads to continued problems. This interactive narrative approach makes the abstract four-step model concrete and memorable by embedding it in a character's journey, showing causality between systematic thinking and successful outcomes. The format reinforces psychology as science by treating problem-solving as a testable process with predictable patterns-when you follow the steps, you get better results.

### **Purpose**: Students navigate a character through a problem-solving journey by voting on which step to use next, experiencing how the four-step model provides structure and reduces overwhelm. This reinforces that systematic problem-solving is more effective than random reactions

### **Placement in Lesson Arc**: Practice C (minutes 31-37). AFTER Practice B (MindMaster Quiz on identifying the four steps). **BEFORE:** Reflection

****Pedagogical Rationale**:** By minute 31, students have seen the four-step model introduced (minutes 5-10), practiced through role-play with peers (minutes 10-20), and taken a knowledge check quiz confirming they can identify and name the steps (minutes 20-26). The game serves as experiential application and integration-students move from "knowing the steps" to "using the steps" in context. The story path format provides scaffolded practice where students experience the full process interactively, seeing how each step connects to the next. The 6-minute duration provides enough time for one complete problem-solving journey with meaningful decision points, positioning this as the capstone practice activity before reflection that consolidates all prior learning.

## In-Class-Game 8C ‚Äì The Brain Gym

## Lesson 3: Thinking of Different Solutions

## LESSON LINK & LEARNING OBJECTIVE

Students will understand that most problems do not have only one "right" answer-there are usually many possible solutions. They will practice generating at least three different solutions to a single problem and comparing them to choose the best fit for the specific situation. They will recognize that creativity, flexibility, and trying new ideas are strengths, not weaknesses, in problem-solving.

## Game file name: icg-08.03-game01.html

## **Game title: The Brain Gym**

## Game format: **Cognitive Challenge Mini-Game (Timed Challenge with generative thinking)**

This format is justified because it emphasizes speed and quantity of idea generation, which directly reinforces flexible thinking-the core skill of Lesson 3. The gym/training metaphor frames the brain's ability to generate multiple solutions as a mental muscle that can be exercised and strengthened. The timed element adds engagement while the multiple-round structure allows students to practice flexibility across different problem types, demonstrating that this cognitive skill generalizes across contexts.

## Purpose: Students practice generating multiple different solutions to various problems within time limits, then compare solutions to determine which fits best. This builds cognitive flexibility, creative thinking, and the understanding that problems typically have many valid approaches-not just one correct answer

**Placement in Lesson**: Practice C (minutes 28-35). AFTER Practice B (Comparing Solutions). Practice C (MindMaster Game). BEFORE Reflection.

**Pedagogical Reasoning:**

- Students first watch creativity video and engage in group brainstorming (minutes 8-20: collaborative idea generation)
- Then practice comparing solutions for "best fit" (minutes 20-28: evaluative thinking)
- Game serves as: Skill intensification and individual mastery check
- By minute 28, students understand that multiple solutions exist and have practiced generating them collaboratively
- Game provides individual practice at speed, building fluency with flexible thinking
- Timed format adds challenge after scaffolded group work
- 7-minute duration allows for 6 training stations with varied mechanics
- Placement allows students to demonstrate independent flexible thinking competency before reflection

## In-Class-Game 8D ‚Äì Puzzle Mountain Expedition

**Lesson 4: Keep Trying and Ask for Help**

### Lesson Link & Learning Objective

Students will learn that problem-solving is rarely perfect the first time and that when a chosen solution doesn't work, persistence-trying again with a different approach-is the appropriate response. They will understand that asking for help is a smart strategy and a sign of strength, not weakness.

### Game File Name: icg-08.04-game01.html

### **Game Title: **Puzzle Mountain Expedition****

### **Game Format**: **Treasure Expedition**

****Format Justification**:** This format allows students to experience persistence and help-seeking through sequential challenge unlocking, where progression requires overcoming obstacles that cannot be solved on the first attempt. Unlike static instruction about persistence, the expedition structure creates authentic struggle moments where initial strategies fail, requiring students to either adjust their approach (persistence) or accept assistance from guide characters (help-seeking) to advance. Each location presents a problem that deliberately resists easy solution, mirroring real problem-solving where first attempts often fail. The unlocking mechanic demonstrates causally that persistence and help-seeking enable progress-students cannot advance without using these strategies, making abstract concepts (try again, ask for help) into concrete, necessary actions with observable results. This experiential format treats persistence as a scientific variable: students test different approaches, observe outcomes, and discover through repeated trials that adjustment and collaboration are effective problem-solving mechanisms, not failures.

### **Purpose**: Students navigate through multiple challenge zones where initial problem-solving attempts fail, requiring them to persist with adjusted strategies or request help from guide characters. This creates experiential understanding that persistence and help-seeking are effective, normal strategies that enable success

### **Placement in Lesson Arc**: Explore A + Practice A (minutes 5-11). AFTER Warm-Up. **BEFORE:** Practice A (Role-Play with Adjustments)

****Pedagogical Rationale**:** The game appears EARLY in the lesson (minutes 5-11) as the foundational experiential learning vehicle. This is intentional-students need to experience failure and persistence before discussing it abstractly. The game serves as the primary data-generation tool: it creates the phenomena (struggling, trying again, seeking help, succeeding) that the lesson then analyzes and names. Following the game (minutes 11-21), students engage in role-plays that reference game experiences: "Remember when you tried the puzzle and it didn't work the first time? That's what this character is experiencing." The quiz (minutes 27-33) and workshop (minutes 33-41) build on insights from game experience. This pedagogical sequence (experience ‚Üí name ‚Üí practice ‚Üí reflect) reflects discovery-based learning where the game generates authentic struggle that becomes the object of study, making persistence and help-seeking concrete before conceptualizing them.

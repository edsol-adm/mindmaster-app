## Home Game 1A ‚Äì Problem Explorer: Journey Through Challenge Island
> Jump to: [Setup](#setup) ¬∑ [Play](#play) ¬∑ [Scoring](#scoring) ¬∑ [Win Condition](#win-condition) ¬∑ [Continuity Note](#continuity-note)

### Learning Objective
Students recognize that problems are normal, everyday experiences that everyone faces. They categorize problems by size (small, medium, larger) and understand that problems are opportunities for the mind to work, not signs of failure.

### Game File Name & Format
`hg-08.01-01.html` ‚Äî Point-and-Click Exploration

### Game Purpose
Reinforce that problems exist everywhere in daily life and can be observed, identified, and categorized. Players explore environments to find problems "in the wild" rather than being presented with them passively, building scientific observation skills.

### Estimated Play Time
8‚Äì10 minutes (30‚Äì50 seconds per problem √ó 12 problems, including exploration time)

---

### Setup
**Opening Screen:**
- Title: "Problem Explorer: Journey Through Challenge Island"
- Background: Colorful map of Challenge Island showing 6 clickable locations: School, Playground, Home, Park, Store, Beach
- Character introduction: Meet Explorer Emma (cartoon child with backpack, binoculars, and explorer hat)
- Instructions: "Problems happen everywhere! Explore Challenge Island. Click on each location to discover what problems people are facing. Then decide: Is it Small, Medium, or Bigger?"
- Problem Size Guide (always visible in corner):
  - üü¢ Small = Easy to solve alone, quick fix
  - üü° Medium = Needs thinking, might involve others
  - üî¥ Bigger = More complex, affects feelings strongly
- Button: "Start Exploring" ‚Üí reveals island map

**UI Elements:**
- Island Map (center): 6 illustrated locations, each clickable
- Discovery Journal (left sidebar): Shows problems discovered so far (0/12)
- Star Counter (top right): "Explorer Stars: 0"
- "Field Guide" button (bottom): Click anytime to review problem size definitions

### Play
**Exploration Phase:**
- **Location Selection:**  
  Player clicks on any of the 6 locations (non-linear‚Äîcan explore in any order)  
  Each location contains 2 problem scenarios to discover  
  Locations unlock with animated "zoom in" effect  

**Problem Discovery (12 total problems across 6 locations):**
- **SCHOOL (2 problems):**  
  Scene A: Click on classroom ‚Üí see child who forgot their pencil (Small)  
  Scene B: Click on hallway ‚Üí see two children both wanting to be line leader (Medium)

- **PLAYGROUND (2 problems):**  
  Scene A: Click on swing set ‚Üí see three kids, only two swings available (Medium)  
  Scene B: Click on sandbox ‚Üí see child's sandcastle accidentally knocked over (Small)

- **HOME (2 problems):**  
  Scene A: Click on bedroom ‚Üí see child can't find their favorite toy (Small)  
  Scene B: Click on living room ‚Üí see siblings arguing over which TV show to watch (Medium)

- **PARK (2 problems):**  
  Scene A: Click on picnic area ‚Üí see family's blanket blowing away in wind (Small)  
  Scene B: Click on path ‚Üí see child feeling nervous about riding bike without training wheels for first time (Bigger)

- **STORE (2 problems):**  
  Scene A: Click on checkout ‚Üí see child dropped their shopping bag, items scattered (Small)  
  Scene B: Click on toy aisle ‚Üí see child wants a toy but parent says no (Medium)

- **BEACH (2 problems):**  
  Scene A: Click on shore ‚Üí see child's hat blew into water (Small)  
  Scene B: Click on volleyball court ‚Üí see group of kids, one child standing alone feeling left out (Bigger)

**Problem Identification Process (per scenario):**
- Animated scene plays (5 seconds) showing the problem  
- Explorer Emma appears: "I discovered a problem! What's happening here?"  
- Player Task: Click "Identify the Problem" button  
- Brief text description appears (e.g., "A child forgot their pencil")  
- Categorization Choice: Three buttons appear:  
  üü¢ Small Problem  
  üü° Medium Problem  
  üî¥ Bigger Problem  
- Player selects one  

**Feedback:**
- Correct categorization:  
  Explorer Emma: "Good observation! [Brief explanation, e.g., 'Yes‚Äîforgetting a pencil is small because it's quick to fix!']"  
  Problem gets recorded in Discovery Journal with correct size icon  
  Earn 2 Explorer Stars ‚≠ê‚≠ê  
- Different but defensible:  
  Explorer Emma: "That's one way to see it! Most explorers said [correct answer]. Different people might see problems differently."  
  Problem recorded, earn 1 Explorer Star ‚≠ê  
- Significantly off:  
  Explorer Emma: "Let me think about that again... [Shows reasoning for correct category]"  
  Problem recorded, earn 1 Explorer Star ‚≠ê (no penalty)

**Return to Map:**  
"Continue Exploring" button ‚Üí returns to island map  
Completed locations show checkmarks (but can be revisited)  
Player continues clicking locations until all 12 problems discovered

**Scientific Frame Reinforcement:**
- After discovering 4 problems: "Scientists observe carefully. You're discovering that problems are everywhere‚Äîat school, home, play!"  
- After discovering 8 problems: "Notice: everyone faces problems. Kids, adults, families. That's normal!"  
- After discovering 12 problems: "You explored like a real problem scientist!"

### Scoring
**Explorer Star System:**
- 2 Stars per correct categorization (24 stars possible)

- **Bonus Discovery Stars:**

### Continuity Note
This game builds on Lesson 1's core message that problems are normal and everywhere. The exploration format puts players in an active scientific role‚Äîobserving, discovering, and classifying‚Äîrather than passively receiving information. This prepares players for Lesson 2 by establishing comfort with problem identification (the "Stop and Notice" step), and sets up the idea that once we notice problems, we can approach them systematically. ## Home Game 1B ‚Äì Mind Toolbox: Match the Problem to the Tool
> Jump to: [Setup](#setup-1) ¬∑ [Play](#play-1) ¬∑ [Scoring](#scoring-1) ¬∑ [Win Condition](#win-condition-1) ¬∑ [Continuity Note](#continuity-note-1)

### Learning Objective
Students shift perspective to see problems as opportunities, not something "bad." They understand that problems are like puzzles meant to be figured out using the mind's tools (thinking, feeling, doing).

### Game File Name & Format
`hg-08.01-02.html` ‚Äî Drag-and-Drop Lab

### Game Purpose
Reinforce that the mind has specific tools (thinking, feeling, doing) that can be applied to problems. Players discover through experimentation which tools work best for different problem types, building scientific hypothesis-testing skills.

### Estimated Play Time
9‚Äì11 minutes (60‚Äì80 seconds per experiment √ó 8 experiments)

---

### Setup
**Opening Screen:**
- Title: "Mind Toolbox: Match the Problem to the Tool"
- Background: Bright laboratory setting with a large workbench in centre
- Character introduction: Meet Lab Scientist Leo (cartoon child wearing lab coat, safety goggles, holding clipboard)
- Instructions: "Your mind has three powerful tools for solving problems! Let's experiment to discover which tools work best for different challenges."
- Display the Three Mind Tools (always visible on left side):
  - üß† Thinking Tool (blue) ‚Äî "Ideas, plans, imagining solutions"
  - üíó Feeling Tool (pink) ‚Äî "Noticing emotions, staying calm, understanding others"
  - üèÉ Doing Tool (green) ‚Äî "Taking action, trying things, practising"
- Button: "Enter the Lab" ‚Üí begins tutorial

**Tutorial (30 seconds):**
- Lab Scientist Leo demonstrates:
  "When I face a problem, I use my Mind Toolbox! Watch: If I can't reach a high shelf, I THINK of using a stool, then I DO it by getting the stool and climbing safely. I used two tools!"
  "Sometimes we need just one tool. Sometimes we need two or all three! Let's experiment and find out."
- Button: "Start Experimenting!" ‚Üí begins game

**UI Elements:**
- Mind Toolbox (left sidebar): Three draggable tool icons (üß†üíóüèÉ)
- Experiment Bench (centre): Shows current problem to solve
- Lab Notes (right sidebar): Records results of experiments
- Gem Counter (top right): "Discovery Gems: 0"
- Experiment Counter (top centre): "Experiment 1 of 8"

---

### Play
**Experimentation Loop (8 problem experiments):**
- **Problem Presentation:**  
  New problem appears on the Experiment Bench (illustrated scene)  
  Examples of 8 problems:  
  1. "You forgot where you put your homework."  
  2. "Your friend is sad because they lost their toy."  
  3. "You want to learn to tie your shoes."  
  4. "You and your sibling both want the last cookie."  
  5. "You feel nervous about going to a new place."  
  6. "Your tower keeps falling down when you build it."  
  7. "You see someone sitting alone at lunch and they look lonely."  
  8. "You can't remember the steps to a game."

**Hypothesis Phase:**
- Lab Scientist Leo: "Which tool or tools do you think will help solve this problem? Drag them to the Experiment Bench!"
- Player sees the three draggable Mind Tools on left
- Task: Drag one, two, or all three tools onto the problem scene
- Tools "land" on the bench and glow when placed
- Can remove tools and try different combinations before testing

**Test Phase:**
- Once player has placed at least one tool, "Run Experiment" button appears  
- Player clicks button  
- Animated "experiment" runs showing how the tool(s) help:
  - Thinking Tool active: Thought bubble appears with solution ideas
  - Feeling Tool active: Heart appears, character shows emotional awareness/calmness
  - Doing Tool active: Character performs action to solve problem

**Results & Feedback:**
- Optimal tool combination selected:  
  Lab Scientist Leo: "Excellent experiment! Those tools worked perfectly together! [Explanation: 'Thinking helped you remember where to look, Doing helped you search.']"  
  Lab Notes update: "‚úì Optimal solution discovered!"  
  Earn 3 Discovery Gems üíéüíéüíé  

- Good but incomplete combination:  
  Lab Scientist Leo: "Good start! Those tools helped. You could also use [missing tool] to make it even better!"  
  Lab Notes update: "‚úì Partial solution found"  
  Earn 2 Discovery Gems üíéüíé  

- Unconventional but creative:  
  Lab Scientist Leo: "Interesting approach! That's creative thinking. Here's another way scientists might solve it..."  
  Lab Notes update: "‚úì Creative attempt"  
  Earn 1 Discovery Gem üíé  

**Scientific Frame Reinforcement:**
- After Experiments 2 and 5: "Scientists test different combinations to see what works! You're discovering which tools your mind needs for different problems."
- After Experiment 7: "Notice: sometimes you need just ONE tool. Other times you need TWO or THREE tools working together. That's flexible problem-solving!"

**Progression:**
- "Next Experiment" button appears ‚Üí loads next problem  
- Previous results stay visible in Lab Notes (scrollable)  
- Repeat for all 8 experiments

## How Lesson 1 Fits into the Full Learning Arc

Lesson 1 launches Unit 8 by reframing **problems as normal parts of life** and introducing the mindset that challenges are opportunities for the mind to work. The two home games extend this concept into active exploration and experimentation: *Problem Explorer* encourages children to identify and classify problems in the world around them, while *Mind Toolbox* transforms abstract mental processes into tangible ‚Äútools‚Äù that can be tested and applied.

- **Lesson 1 (this document):** Normalises problems as everyday occurrences and introduces the idea that the mind‚Äôs tools‚Äîthinking, feeling, and doing‚Äîare resources for solving them.  
- **Lesson 2:** Teaches the structured four-step problem-solving model (Stop and Notice ‚Üí Think of Options ‚Üí Choose One ‚Üí Try It Out), giving students a reliable process to follow.  
- **Lesson 3:** Expands on flexible thinking and creativity, showing that there is often more than one good solution to a problem.  
- **Lesson 4:** Builds persistence and social support skills, reframing ‚Äúasking for help‚Äù as a strength rather than weakness.

> üß† **Learning Arc Summary:** Recognising Problems ‚Üí Understanding Tools ‚Üí Applying Steps ‚Üí Building Resilience.  
> Through this arc, students shift from *noticing that problems exist* to *confidently engaging with them* using structured, flexible, and persistent approaches. By the end of the unit, they understand that problem-solving is both a cognitive and emotional skill‚Äîrooted in observation, experimentation, and the belief that ‚ÄúI can handle challenges.‚Äù

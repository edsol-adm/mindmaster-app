### Learning Objective
Students explain that most problems have multiple possible solutions. They generate at least three different solutions through brainstorming and demonstrate flexible thinking by remaining open to different approaches.

### Game File Name & Format

### Game Title

### Game Purpose

### Continuity Note
This game emphasizes the speed and quantity aspect of brainstorming‚Äîteaching players that generating MANY solutions quickly is a valuable skill. Unlike Game 3A (Solution Factory) which focused on matching solution types thoughtfully through a puzzle mechanic, this game rewards rapid idea generation and diversity through timed pressure.

Together, the two Lesson 3 games show:
- Game 3A: Thoughtful categorization and pattern-matching of solution types  
- Game 3B: Fast, prolific idea generation without self-censoring  

This dual approach teaches that flexible thinking involves:
- Quantity (generating many options‚Äîthis game)  
- Quality (recognizing different solution categories‚ÄîGame 3A)  

Preparation for Lesson 4: This game directly prepares players for Lesson 4's persistence concepts. Having practiced planting many "seeds" (solution options), players understand that when their first choice doesn't work, they have backup plans‚Äîdemonstrating persistence through having generated alternatives. The garden metaphor reinforces that problem-solving is about cultivation: planting many seeds increases the chance that some will flourish.

**Scientific Framework:** The game is grounded in creativity research showing that:
- Divergent thinking (generating multiple ideas) precedes convergent thinking (selecting best option)  
- Quantity often leads to quality in brainstorming  
- Time pressure can enhance creative output by reducing self-censorship  
- Diverse idea types (practical, creative, collaborative) strengthen problem-solving

## How Lesson 3 Fits into the Full Learning Arc

Lesson 3 expands the Unit 8 problem-solving framework by deepening the *Think of Options* stage of the four-step model. Whereas Lesson 2 focused on applying structured steps, Lesson 3 helps children discover that strong problem-solvers use imagination and flexibility to create more than one possible answer. The two home games reinforce this from complementary angles: **Solution Factory** develops deliberate, pattern-based thinking for identifying solution types, while **Idea Sprout** builds fluency and speed in generating diverse ideas. Together they shift children from *following steps* to *thinking creatively within steps.*

- **Lesson 1:** Normalises problems as everyday challenges and introduces the idea that they can be approached using the mind‚Äôs tools.  
- **Lesson 2:** Teaches the structured four-step model (Stop ‚Üí Think ‚Üí Choose ‚Üí Try) that turns confusion into action.  
- **Lesson 3 (this document):** Strengthens the *Think of Options* step through flexible, imaginative solution-generation, showing that most problems have more than one right answer.  
- **Lesson 4:** Builds persistence and help-seeking, showing that when one idea doesn‚Äôt work, having multiple options allows continued progress.

> üß† **Learning Arc Summary:** From recognising problems to generating and testing solutions, Lesson 3 transforms problem-solving from a fixed sequence into a creative exploration. By practising both deliberate and rapid idea generation, students strengthen cognitive flexibility‚Äîthe bridge between structured thinking and resilient action.
